Partition Plan of Mandatory Palestine and the 1947-1948 Conflict

Background

Following World War II and the Holocaust, there was increased international support for the establishment of a Jewish state in Palestine. Tensions between Jewish and Arab communities escalated.

UN Partition Plan (1947)

On November 29, 1947, the United Nations General Assembly adopted Resolution 181, proposing to partition Palestine into separate Jewish and Arab states, with Jerusalem as an international city. The plan allocated approximately 55% of the territory to the Jewish state and 45% to the Arab state.

Reactions to the Partition Plan

The Jewish community accepted the plan, viewing it as a step toward statehood. In contrast, the Arab community rejected it, opposing any division of Palestine and asserting their rights to the land.

Outbreak of Conflict (1947-1948)

Following the adoption of the UN plan, violence erupted between Jewish and Arab groups. Clashes intensified, leading to a civil war in the months leading up to the British withdrawal.

End of the British Mandate and Declaration of Independence (1948)

The British withdrew from Palestine on May 14, 1948. On the same day, David Ben-Gurion declared the establishment of the State of Israel, leading to immediate conflict with neighboring Arab states.
